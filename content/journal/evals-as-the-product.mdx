If you cannot tell, on a given Tuesday, whether your AI feature is better or worse than it was last Friday — you do not have a product, you have a prompt. The discipline that closes that gap is evals. Not benchmarks, not vibe checks. A versioned suite of inputs and graders that runs every time anything changes.

## The cheapest possible eval is still better than no eval

Teams stall here because they imagine they need a 10,000-row gold set before they can start. They do not. The first useful eval suite is fifty hand-written prompts and an LLM-as-judge with a one-paragraph rubric. It will catch 80% of regressions for two weeks of effort.

> Every model swap, every prompt edit, every retrieval rebuild — runs the suite first. No suite, no merge. That is the rule.

## What good rubrics look like

A good grader is a paragraph, not a score. Teach the grader the *failure modes* you want caught:

- Hallucinated entities that don't appear in the source corpus.
- Citations that point to the wrong paragraph.
- Refusals that leak policy text the user shouldn't see.
- Answers that technically satisfy the question but miss the user's obvious intent.

An LLM judge reading that rubric will outperform a five-point Likert score every time.

## Where evals stop being optional

Once you cross 1,000 active users, the eval suite is the only thing standing between you and a regression that costs you trust. We treat the suite the way SREs treat error budgets. Falling below the bar blocks deploys. Engineers triage the failures the way they triage incidents.

If you want a single lever to pull on AI product quality this quarter, it is this one.
