Every serious team we have onboarded in the past year asks the same question on day one: should we move to RSC? The answer in the abstract is yes. The answer in practice is — only if you are willing to treat it as an infrastructure migration, not a refactor.

## What the demos hide

The marketing pages for Server Components show a single component flipping from client to server with a one-line change. Real codebases discover, three weeks in, that the boundary lives at the data layer — and the data layer is the part nobody had time to clean up.

> RSC is not a render strategy. It is a question about who owns secrets, who owns latency, and who owns the cache.

## The four boundaries that actually matter

- Auth and session — moves fully server, but only if your auth provider has a Node SDK that supports streaming.
- Tracking and consent — needs an explicit client island, otherwise you ship analytics scripts to bots.
- Forms and mutations — Server Actions only beat REST when you wire optimistic UI with `useOptimistic`.
- Third-party widgets — most chat, payment, and embed scripts assume `window`. Wrap them or rebuild them.

Until those four are decided in writing, RSC will save you nothing. After they are decided, the size budget speaks for itself: we have shipped two trading interfaces under 200KB this year, both on Next.js 16 with Fluid Compute. Neither team had to abandon a single library — they had to be honest about which library belonged on which side of the boundary.

## A pragmatic checklist

Before your team writes a single `'use client'` directive, run this:

1. Enumerate every piece of client state and label it `session`, `ephemeral`, or `derived`.
2. Look at every third-party `<script>` and decide: server-proxied, client-islanded, or removed.
3. Audit your data fetches — which ones can move to the server *now*, and which are blocked on SDK gaps?

If the answers are clear, the migration will take three weeks. If they aren't, stop and make them clear before you touch a line of component code.
